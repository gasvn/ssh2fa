01 how it works

Type your 2FA once. Then just ssh.

[01]

Import your hosts

SSH2FA reads your ~/.ssh/config and lets you enable hosts in one click — or add one by hand and scan its 2FA QR. Credentials live in the macOS Keychain; a test login runs before saving.

[02]

It stays warm

The daemon keeps two SSH ControlMaster connections alive per host, and auto-recovers them after sleep, network changes, and reboots.

[03]

Connect instantly

Run ssh <alias> in your own terminal — it reuses the warm connection, no password, no code. SSH2FA wires up the reuse for you (one consented line in your ssh config); you edit nothing.

02 features

Built for the connection you live in.

Stays logged in

Two warm ControlMaster connections per host mean every ssh, scp, and editor remote reuses an open channel — instant, with no second factor to type.

Your terminal — zero config

Your own ssh <alias> reuses the warm master too, with no 2FA re-prompt. SSH2FA wires it up via one consented, backed-up line in your ssh config — and never touches your own Host blocks.

Answers 2FA for you

Submits your password and a fresh TOTP code at login — serialized so a shared Duo secret is never replayed inside its window.

Imports your ssh config

Reads ~/.ssh/config and lets you enable existing hosts in one click — or scan a 2FA QR to add the secret. No double entry.

Self-healing

Adopts live connections across restarts and rebuilds them after sleep / wake — zero re-login.

SLURM forwarding + live countdown

Forward a local port to a running job's compute node (Jupyter, dashboards) — with a live, color-coded expiry countdown from squeue and a heads-up before your allocation ends.

Security, stated plainly

SSH2FA stores your SSH password and TOTP secret in the macOS Keychain and submits the second factor for you. That's the convenience — but it means your "second factor" no longer protects you against someone with access to your unlocked Mac. Read the full security model before you rely on it.
